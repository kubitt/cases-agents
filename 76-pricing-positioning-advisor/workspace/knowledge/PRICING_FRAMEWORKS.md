## Value-based pricing
Price on the value the customer gets, not on your cost. If your tool saves an agency $2,000/mo, a $200/mo price is easy to justify. This is the primary lens; cost only sets the floor. ARPU (average revenue per user) is the average monthly revenue you earn per paying customer — a 10% ARPU lift is often easier to win than a batch of new customers.

## Cost-plus sanity floor
Never price below what delivery costs plus your target margin: floor = unit cost / (1 − target margin). It does not set the price — it stops you from pricing into a loss. Always the floor, never the goal.

## Tiered good-better-best
Offer three tiers. Most buyers pick the middle one, so design the middle as the plan you want most people on. The cheap tier lowers the entry barrier; the top tier makes the middle look reasonable.

## Willingness-to-pay (Van Westendorp)
Gauge, by asking customers or reading the market, at what price the product feels too cheap, cheap, expensive, and too expensive. The overlap of those answers suggests an acceptable range. With no survey data, approximate it from where competitors cluster and from the value delivered.

## Anchoring and the decoy effect
The first number a buyer sees frames the rest (anchoring). A deliberately less-attractive higher tier (a decoy) makes the tier you want them on feel like the smart choice. Use honestly — never invent a tier no one can actually buy.

## Positioning canvas (April Dunford style)
Five parts: category (what kind of thing you are), target segment (for whom), competitive alternative (what they use instead), unique value (what only you credibly offer), and proof (the evidence). Fill all five before writing a positioning statement.

## When each applies
- Setting or raising a price → value-based first, cost-plus as the floor, willingness-to-pay for the range.
- Packaging or adding plans → tiered good-better-best plus anchoring.
- Standing out / messaging → the positioning canvas.
